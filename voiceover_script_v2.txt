Ever wanted to filter out the corporate BS?

The job posting that said fast-paced and meant understaffed. The investor who said really rooting for you and meant goodbye. The performance review that said leadership potential and meant not this cycle.

Introducing Subtext. A Chrome extension that reads between the lines.

[BEAT 1 - INVESTOR EMAIL]

Here is a real VC rejection email. Marcus is, quote, really rooting for you.

Subtext gives it an 84 out of 100 on the BS score.

The archetype: The Eternal Encourager. Someone who has never once said a hard thing directly in their professional life.

Translation of really rooting for you: goodbye.

The reply you actually want to send: Hi Marcus. What does excited about the space mean in a world where you just passed? Asking for my runway.

[BEAT 2 - DOORDASH JOB POSTING]

DoorDash. Work hard and play hard. No job is beneath you. In-office snacks.

Survival probability: twenty-two percent.

No job is beneath you means: we will ask you to do things that are beneath you, constantly, and act surprised when you quit.

Health insurance, employee only, dependents not covered: the cold brew in the fridge is what we offer instead of maternity coverage.

[BEAT 3 - HONEYCOMB JOB POSTING]

Honeycomb. A passionate group of bees. Obsessed with craft.

Survival probability: thirty-one percent.

Passionate group of bees means: you will work with people who believe intensity is a personality trait.

Honest rewrite: we move fast and care deeply. We have not quite figured out what healthy limits look like. You will be expected to share our values, which are mostly about working more.

[REAL WORLD USE CASES]

Here is why this matters.

Twenty-eight percent of US workers are actively looking for a new job right now. More than a quarter of job postings contain at least one red-flag phrase — up from eighteen percent just eight years ago. Every one of those people is trying to decode what the posting actually means.

The average founder pitches fifty-eight investors to close one seed round. VCs fund about one percent of what they see. Almost none of the rejections explain why. Founders spend weeks interpreting emails like Marcus's instead of improving their pitch.

Ninety-five percent of managers say they are frustrated with their own review systems. Fifty percent of employees receive feedback that is not actionable. Only fourteen percent say performance reviews actually motivate them to improve.

Two million LinkedIn posts go up every single day. Most of them are performance, not information.

Everybody knows the language is empty. Now nobody has to decode it alone.
